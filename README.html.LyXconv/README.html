1 Order Book Liquidity

There are mainly two groups of liquidity providers in the market, namely the market makers and the limit order traders[3]. Harris and Hasbrouck provide evidence that placing limit orders at or better than the prevailing quotes is a better trading strategy than simply using market orders, even after accounting for non-execution risks[2].
The cost-to-trade measures gauge the ability of the limit order book to handle a sudden surge in the demand for liquidity by market orders.
The deeper the book is, the less it penalizes liquidity demanders[3].

1.1 Close to Maturity

When we’re close to maturity, the by no-aribtrage (which assumes lack of liquidity constraints), the call-option price would be ST − K whenever option is in the money. For an in-the-money option, our hedged portfolio moves asΠT = αBT + Δ⋅ST. The value of the two should be the same and Δ should thus approach unity. If the option is out of money, then ST > K and the price of the option should be zero (no position in stock necessary as the out-of-the-money option is worthless around maturity when stock-price remains constant).
